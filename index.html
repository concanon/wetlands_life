Red Foxes were introduced near some wetlands for fox hunting and fur production. These expert hunters often predate on native birds and small animals.  
 
 
  Surprisingly, pet and feral House Cats stalk the wetlands preying on birds, mice, lizards and other small animals. Cat guardians who live near wetlands should keep their pets indoors and provide adequate food to prevent the cats from hunting native species.  
 
 
  Some snails accidentally brought from other parts of the world eat wetlands plants and have few predators to keep them in check!  
 
 
  Rare and beautiful Legless Lizards burrow beneath the sand, eating insects in the wetlands. Although they have no legs and appear similar to snakes, they are evolutionarily descended from lizards.  
 
 
  Cottontail rabbits can sometimes be glimpsed bounding away for cover of brush as one treks through the wetlands.  
 
 
  Silver Dune Lupine has characteristic fuzzy silver leaves, purple flowers, and seed pods.  
 
 
  Pickleweed is a unique succulent adapted to live in salty soil. It is home and food for insects, birds and other organisms.  
 
 
  Branching Phacelia grows in wetland meadows and slopes and has an unusual, beautiful appearance.  
 
 
  If you look closely at the carpet of white flowers covering some wetland areas, you'll see it's made of Heliotrope.  
 
 
  Willow was used by native peoples for tools, construction and medicinal purposes.  
 
 
  Euphorbia is a quickly spreading invasive plant with white sap.  
 
 
  Foxtails have invaded large regions of wetlands, with an annoying habit of sticking in people's socks! They also prevent native plants from growing.  
 
 
  Iceplant which is originally from Africa has adapted to live in some wetlands. It grows like an island of green french fries, drowning out other plants.  
 
 
  Estuary waters bordering the wetlands are nurseries for a vast variety of fish, aquatic invertibrates and other marine life.  
 
 
  Eelgrass grows underwater, where it is home for fish and provides food for thousands of tiny organisms. It is important to our ocean's overall health.  
 
 
  An immediate solution to plastic debris in the ocean is for humans to develop biodegradable plastic alternatives. Plastics in the ocean and shores are a serious problem as many animals are killed by unknowingly eating human-made materials. Volunteer cleanup and purchasing biodegradable materials will also help.  
 
 
  Native Bumblebees are important pollinators!  
 
 
  Beautiful snow-white Egrets can be seen hunting or flying overhead.  
 
 
  Majestic Great Blue Herons float over the wetlands with wingspans close to 7 feet long!
